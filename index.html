Our Mission

A Voice to the Voiceless

Here at P.A.C.T. (Paraclete Advocacy for Children & Teens), we are guided by the love and justice of Christ. Our mission is to be a Voice to the Voiceless—serving children and teens from disadvantaged communities who often face the court system alone.

Through prayerfully trained volunteers, we walk alongside each child with compassion and purpose, advocating for their dignity, healing, and future. With God as our foundation, we strive to break cycles of silence and suffering, planting seeds of hope that grow into generations of faithful, whole-hearted servants.

The Urgent Need

Why P.A.C.T. Matters

Children in Crisis: Unheard Voices

Each year, over 600,000 children in the U.S. experience abuse or neglect. Many enter a complex foster care or legal system where their voices go unheard.

600K+ Children Abused / Neglected
~97K Advocates Available

For every 600,000 children, there are only around 97,000 advocates—leaving thousands without someone to stand beside them.

What happens to these children when no one speaks up?

How many more have to suffer before we rise up as a community?

Aging Out: A Harsh Reality

Every year, about 23,000 youth age out of foster care. The statistics are heartbreaking:

50% Homeless / Unemployed (1st yr)
38% Homeless by 26
30% Law Run-ins by 21
~70% Men Incarcerated (mid-20s)

They lack guidance, mentors, stable housing, mental health services, and real-life skills. That's exactly what P.A.C.T. is here to provide—a voice of compassion and healing while bridging the gaps between families, communities, and the system.

My Story

Why I Advocate

For me, this mission is personal. My niece, Kloe, lost her life tragically at the age of 3. Child Protective Services had been in and out of the home for her and her sister—but no one took decisive action. No one spoke up. She fell through the cracks.

I know what it's like to be unheard within the court system. I had to redo interviews simply because they weren't listening. I was returned to abusive environments where my safety was in jeopardy. The result? A rapid decline in my mental health, my faith shaken, and a complete distrust in the system that was supposed to protect me.

I speak now for Kloe, for myself, and for the countless children like Gabriel Fernandez, Jhessye Shockley, Zymere Perkins, Adrian Jones, Harmony Montgomery—young lives lost because they were ignored.

As a child advocate, I want to be that consistent voice. Research shows that when a child has an advocate—like a CASA volunteer—they are more likely to receive critical services, succeed in school, and be placed in safe, permanent homes faster.

This isn't just meaningful work. It's necessary.

The system is overwhelmed, and far too many children—especially those from marginalized communities—fall through the cracks. I want to be part of closing those gaps, and ensuring that every child feels seen, protected, and empowered.
